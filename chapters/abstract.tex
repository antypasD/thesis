Social media has become an integral part of daily life for millions worldwide, serving as a platform where people exchange opinions, express concerns, and influence one another. From natural disasters to political debates, social media platforms like X (formerly Twitter) and Reddit have become primary sources of news and public discourse. This activity results in the generation of vast amounts of data each day which, due to their sheer volume and largely unstructured nature, researchers and analysts face significant challenges in effectively studying and analysing it.

To analyse this complex data source, researchers frequently utilise \ac{NLP} tools and techniques. However, social media data poses unique challenges distinct from more formal or structured text. The landscape of social media is constantly evolving, with new slang, trending topics, emojis, and brevity of posts creating additional hurdles that traditional \ac{NLP} methods often struggle to address.

The emergence of  \acp{LLM}, such as GPT-3 \cite{GPT3}, has transformed the landscape of natural language processing, making it possible to analyse and generate text at an unprecedented scale. However, our findings indicate that smaller, specialized models can often perform competitively with, and occasionally outperform, these larger models on domain-specific tasks.  significant when considering the resource and environmental costs associated with training and deploying large-scale models, underscoring the importance of efficient, task-oriented model development.

In this work, we introduce several domain-specific models and datasets tailored specifically for social media data. We apply these specialized tools to studies focusing on politically charged content, hate speech detection, and misinformation. By leveraging these resources, we aim to demonstrate that specialized models not only offer competitive performance but also provide a more sustainable approach to social media data analysis.
