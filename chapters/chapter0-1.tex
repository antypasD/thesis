\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:intro} % Add a label in case you want to refer to the chapter.

The rise of social media platforms has transformed the way people communicate and interact online. Platforms like \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}, have grown to be an integral part of the lives of millions of people, with approximately 63\% of the population using a social media platform daily \footnote{https://www.statista.com/statistics/617136/digital-population-worldwide/}, and are seen as key sources of real-time information, public opinion, and social dynamics, making them valuable data sources for understanding human behaviour and societal trends \cite{barbera2015birds,shu2017fake}. As populations shape opinions and influence each other through these platforms \cite{ausat2023role}, it has become crucial, from both business and scientific perspectives, to better understand how people interact and what factors drive their behaviours online.

This need becomes even more apparent in the context of political discourse, given how easily voters can be influenced and manipulated through targeted social media campaigns \citet{goodwin2023political}. For example, \citet{zhang2025trump} demonstrates how President Trump’s often incendiary posts on Twitter and Truth Social amplified social media user engagement and attracted increased news media attention. Similar studies on other major political events, such as Brexit \cite{mora2021influence}, highlight the pivotal role social media platforms play in shaping how information spreads through networks, facilitating content generation, and influencing user behaviour.

To effectively leverage this rich but chaotic data, \ac{NLP} provides an essential set of tools for extracting, structuring, and analysing textual content from social media. Standard tasks include sentiment analysis \cite{zimbra2018state}, topic modelling \cite{egger2022topic}, and entity recognition \cite{nasar2021named}, which help quantify emotional tone, uncover underlying themes, and identify key actors within discussions. Recent advances in neural architectures, such as transformers \cite{subakan2021attention}, enable deeper contextual understanding and have paved the way for more nuanced tasks, including stance detection \cite{aldayel2021stance}, misinformation tracking \cite{hayawi2022anti}, and aspect-based sentiment analysis \cite{brauwers2022survey}. These methods allow researchers to convert raw, unstructured posts into structured formats that support downstream analysis.

Furthermore, social media data allows for tracking of user behaviour, discourse evolution, and community dynamics \cite{feuerriegel2025using, arazzi2023importance}. Through the use of \ac{NLP} techniques, it becomes possible to detect coordinated campaigns, assess the spread of narratives, or evaluate public reactions over time \cite{luceri2024leveraging}. These insights are increasingly valuable in fields such as computational social science, marketing, and policy analysis, where understanding the connections between language, behaviour, and influence is crucial. The intersection of \ac{NLP} and social media offers significant opportunities for both methodological innovation and impactful, real-world applications.

Despite their potential, analysing social media data poses numerous challenges for the field of \ac{NLP}. User-generated content on these platforms often lacks context and is highly informal, containing slang, emojis, abbreviations, typos, and other forms of noisy data \cite{eisenstein2013bad,gain-etal-2023-reference}. Moreover, language usage on social media evolves rapidly, making it difficult for static models and lexicons to keep pace. These characteristics demand robust, adaptive, and context-aware approaches to effectively process and interpret the vast and dynamic stream of social media text.

In order to tackle the limitations above, we explore existing methodologies and resources that work on understanding complex social interactions on social media. Subsequently, we introduce new models and datasets that improve on previous work, apply them on large corpora collected from \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit} to better understand human interactions regarding politics discourse, and misinformation and hate speech proliferation. The rest of this introductory Chapter is organised as follows: \Autoref{ch1:motivation} outlines the research aim and motivations for this thesis,  \Autoref{ch1:contributions} outlines the contribution of research, \Autoref{ch:intro:publications} lists publications that are peer-reviewed or currently under review, and \Autoref{ch1:outline} describes the structure of the thesis and summarises this introductory Chapter.

\section{Motivation} \label{ch1:motivation}
\ac{NLP} applications are essential for understanding human interactions in social media, especially in the context of political discourse and the dissemination of misinformation and hate speech detection. For example, \ac{ML} models are used by social platforms, such as Facebook \cite{schroepfer2021update}, to detect harmful content and protect users from online abuse. Similarly, researchers have developed tools to analyse political communication on Twitter \cite{barbera2015birds}, track the spread of misinformation \cite{shu2017fake}, and understand the dynamics of online discussions \cite{zhang2018conversations}. 

\dimos{While the emergence of more advanced transformer-based like the RoBERTA models and other more \acp{LLM} like LLaMA, the majority of the research in the social sciences still relies on older lexicon-based methods, such as VADER \cite{hutto2014vader}, for tasks such as sentiment analysis, topic framing, and hate speech detection \cite{shapiro2017politicians}. While these methods are simple to apply and interpret, they often struggle to capture the complexity, nuance, and platform-specific linguistic variation of social media language \cite{kovacs2021challenges}. This highlights the importance of providing social scientists, and other non \ac{NLP} experts, with accurate and easy-to-use tools that can be evaluated and customised for specific research questions, without requiring deep technical expertise. However, developing such models is a challenging task as it often involves the need for annotated data tailored to the target domain, but also the ability to conduct clear and consistent evaluations to ensure their reliability.}

Many hate speech detection models rely on supervised learning techniques, which require large amounts of annotated data to train effectively. However, the availability of high-quality annotated datasets for hate speech detection is limited, and existing datasets often suffer from biases and inconsistencies \cite{davidson2017automated}. This can lead to models that are not generalisable across different contexts or languages, limiting their effectiveness in real-world applications. Other applications, such as misinformation detection and sentiment analysis, also face similar challenges in terms of data availability and quality. For example, misinformation detection models often rely on large-scale datasets of social media posts, which can be difficult to obtain and may not accurately reflect the diversity of online content \cite{shu2017fake}. Similarly, sentiment analysis models often struggle to capture the nuances of social media language, which can vary significantly across different platforms and user demographics \cite{barbieri2021xlm}.

Due to the heavily unstructured content often shared in social media content, many of the aforementioned applications rely on specialised datasets to perform well. These datasets are often collected from social media platforms, such as \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}, and annotated by human coders to provide ground truth labels for training and evaluation. However, the quality and reliability of these datasets can vary significantly, depending on the annotation process and the expertise of the annotators \cite{klie2024analyzing}. This can lead to biases and inaccuracies in the models trained on these datasets, limiting their generalisability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios. 

The proliferation of social media platforms has created unprecedented challenges in political discourse and misinformation spread \cite{muhammed2022disaster}. Political polarisation and the rapid dissemination of false information have become major societal concerns, affecting democratic processes and public opinion formation \cite{allcott2017social}. These challenges are further complicated by the heavily unstructured nature of social media content, which can pose difficulties even for larger models \cite{eisenstein2013bad,gain-etal-2023-reference}. Finally, it is crucial to recognise both the potential biases in existing models, tools, and datasets, as well as the environmental impact associated with developing and deploying \acp{LLM} \cite{bender2021dangers}.

\section{Contributions} \label{ch1:contributions}
 
The main motivation of this thesis is to explore how \ac{NLP} techniques can be effectively applied to social media data in order to address complex societal questions. Social media platforms generate vast amounts of unstructured textual data that offer unique insights into public opinion, communication patterns, and societal events. However, leveraging this data poses significant challenges due to its informal language, high variability, and noise. This raises a central research question: How can NLP methodologies be adapted or developed to robustly analyse and interpret social media content for meaningful societal insights? This question branches into more specific problems concerning data representation, model design, annotation strategies, and evaluation practices.

Rather than attempting to solve all aspects of the problem, this thesis aims to fill gaps in existing literature on concrete sub-problems where progress can be made on how to detect subtle patterns in public discourse, how to model social dynamics from textual evidence, and how to evaluate such models in realistic settings. Specifically, the research presented in this thesis focuses on two core areas: (1) developing and evaluating \ac{NLP} resources for social media analysis; and (2) applying these resources through well-defined approaches to analyse social media dynamics in the context of political discourse, misinformation spread and hate speech propagation. By addressing these areas, the thesis aims to provide new insights into the dynamics of social media communication and contribute to the development of more effective tools for understanding and combating harmful online behaviours. To this end, the thesis makes a number of noticeable contributions as listed below:

\subsection{Part 1: \PARTONE} \label{ch:intro:contributions}
The first core contribution of the thesis focuses on developing and evaluating \ac{NLP} tools specifically designed for social media applications. \label{ch:intro:contributions:1}

\begin{enumerate}[label=I.\arabic*]
    \item \label{contribution:1} \textbf{New Datasets for Topic Classification on Twitter.} Extracting meaningful insights from the vast amount of data generated on Twitter is inherently challenging, and performing targeted topic analysis adds further complexity. Unsupervised methods such as topic modelling are often difficult to evaluate and interpret, while existing datasets for supervised classification are not well-suited for the informal and dynamic nature of social media content. To address this gap, we introduce two novel datasets, {\TweetTopic} and its multilingual extension {\XTOPIC}, specifically designed for training and evaluating topic classification models on Twitter data. These datasets, along with corresponding \ac{SoTA} models, are detailed in Chapters~\ref{ch:tweet-topic} and~\ref{ch:x-topic} \dimos{respectively}. 
    \item \label{contribution:2} \dimos{\textbf{Cross-Dataset Analysis of Hate Speech Detection Resources.} Although hate speech detection is a widely studied problem, many available datasets lack diversity and tend to focus on specific target groups, limiting the generalisability of trained models. We present a cross-dataset analysis of existing hate speech detection datasets, highlighting their limitations and the need for more comprehensive resources. Building on this analysis, we introduce a unified hate speech detection dataset specifically curated for social media content by systematically standardising and aggregating existing resources. This dataset is detailed in \Autoref{ch:hate-speech}, where we also demonstrate how it can be used to train models that outperform existing approaches.}
    \item \label{contribution:3} \textbf{Introduction of a Benchmark for Twitter-related \dimos{NLP} Tasks.} We present {\DATASET}, a benchmark that unifies and standardises the evaluation of social media \ac{NLP} tasks, addressing the current fragmentation in this area. Comprising \dimos{12} diverse tasks, our benchmark poses significant challenges not only for smaller \acp{PLM} but also for the latest \acp{LLM}, as discussed in \Autoref{ch:super-tweeteval}.
\end{enumerate} 

A common observation across all three contributions is that smaller, specialised models, such as RoBERTa, can perform as well as, and often outperform, larger \acp{LLM} like GPT-4 on the evaluated tasks when fine-tuned. This observation is primarily due to the specialised training data: the models we evaluated are frequently trained on large corpora of social media content, making them better suited for domain-specific language use and nuances. Additionally, their architectural design contributes to this advantage. Unlike generative \acp{LLM}, which are based on a decoder architecture, smaller \acp{PLM} typically employ an encoder architecture, which is inherently more effective for discriminative tasks such as classification. Encoders focus on learning contextual representations of input sequences, which allows for precise feature extraction\footnote{Chapter \ref{ch:background:language_models} provides details on the architecture and processing mechanisms of encoder-based models.} and robust classification performance, which is particularly important for tasks like topic classification and hate speech detection, where subtle linguistic cues and contextual dependencies are critical.

The impact of this thesis is evidenced by the widespread adoption of the released datasets and models. For instance, the topic classification resources introduced in Chapters \ref{ch:tweet-topic} and \ref{ch:x-topic} have accumulated over 1,200,000 downloads on the Hugging Face platform\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/collections/cardiffnlp/tweettopic-65eb2a0eada92a05d3d103ce}}, while our hate speech classifier (\Autoref{ch:hate-speech}) has more than 1,400,000 downloads\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/cardiffnlp/twitter-roberta-base-hate-latest/settings}}, highlighting their practical value and community uptake.

Apart from the wide adoption of the resources, the models and datasets have been used in various studies from different domains. For example, they have informed research in tourism studies, where \citet{lee2025framing} employed our topic classification model to perform a targeted investigation on online hostility directed at tourists in the context of over-tourism debates. Similarly, our hate speech classifier (\Autoref{ch:hate-speech}) has demonstrated performance comparable to, or surpassing, established tools such as Google’s Perspective API\footnote{\url{https://perspectiveapi.com/}} and OpenAI’s Moderation system\footnote{\url{https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/moderation}}, as shown by \citet{shen2025hatebench}. It has also been adopted by researchers to examine the temporal dynamics of hate speech on Twitter \citet{arun2024x}, and to investigate correlations between hate speech and crime statistics \cite{bicakci2025big}.

This interdisciplinary application underscores the utility of robust \ac{NLP} tools in social science and humanities contexts, as evidenced by citations our papers have achieved across computer science, linguistics, political science, psychology, business, environmental science, medicine, and philosophy\footnote{According to data from \url{https://www.semanticscholar.org/}}.


\subsection{Part 2: \PARTTWO} \label{ch:intro:contributions:part2}
We use the resources developed and insights extracted in the previous Chapters to analyse complex social media dynamics.

\begin{enumerate}[label=II.\arabic*]
    \item  \label{contribution:4}\textbf{Politics and Negativity.} We conduct a large-scale analysis of posts by politicians from eight different parliaments across five languages (including English, Spanish, and Greek) to investigate how negatively charged tweets tend to spread more widely and rapidly than neutral or positive ones. To achieve this, we train domain-specific sentiment analysis models and develop a systematic approach to quantify and track the diffusion of such content. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:political}.
    \item \label{contribution:5} \textbf{Profiling Misinformation Spreaders.} We present a methodology to profile users who frequently share misinformation on social media. By leveraging our previously developed models for topic classification and hate speech detection, along with sentiment and emotion analysis, we systematically analyse a large number of user accounts to uncover behavioural patterns and content characteristics associated with misinformation spread. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:spreaders}.
    \item \label{contribution:6} \textbf{Trigger Points Analysis.} Trigger points are a relatively new concept referring to specific words or phrases that can provoke strong emotional reactions in discourse. \dimos{We aim to validate the concept of “trigger words” and demonstrate how they can increase negative sentiment, evoke strong emotions, and contribute to the emergence of hate speech in online discussions.}. Unlike previous qualitative studies that introduced the notion of trigger points, our approach adopts a quantitative perspective. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:triggers}.
\end{enumerate}


Throughout this work, we provide detailed and transparent methodologies and approaches that are not only robust but also easy to replicate and adapt. The approaches we introduce are designed to be easily applicable to other datasets and platforms beyond Twitter and Reddit, enabling researchers and practitioners to extend our analyses to a wide range of social media sources and related contexts.

Finally, it is worth noting that there was a consistent effort to collaborate with experts from other fields, such as social sciences and healthcare, and to incorporate their feedback in order to address real-world problems through our research. These interdisciplinary collaborations,  as seen in \Autoref{ch1:sec:collaboration}, highlight the practical relevance of our work.

\section{Ethics and Privacy}
\dimos{Protecting the privacy of users as well as following established ethical rules is crucial in social media research, as users often share personal information and express opinions on sensitive topics such as politics, health, and social issues. Conducting research in this domain requires particular care to protect individuals' privacy and to avoid unintended harm. This is especially critical when dealing with content related to hate speech and misinformation, as results can be potentially misused or misinterpreted, leading to further harm or perpetuating harmful narratives.}

The research included in the thesis has been approved by Cardiff University's ethics committee and also follows the guidelines of the ACL code of ethics\footnote{https://www.acm.org/code-of-ethics}.

Specifically, as our research focuses on social media, we have taken caution while collecting data used in our experiments to adhere to the aforementioned ethic rules. Firstly, all the data were collected using the official \acp{API} provided by \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}. Moreover, the privacy of the users whose data is used is protected by masking user handles and \acp{URL} to prevent any potential identification of users. Exceptions are data collected from  verified\footnote{As defined by the legacy verified policy of Twitter in https://help.x.com/en/managing-your-account/legacy-verification-policy.} \textit{Twitter} users who are already public figures such as politicians, celebrities, and government employees.

Finally, regarding the annotations conducted, all external coders\footnote{In Chapters \ref{ch:political} and \ref{ch:triggers}, annotations were conducted by members of the research team.} were paid at least minimum wage for their work, and no personal identifying information was collected. Further details on the ethical considerations and privacy measures taken in this research can be found in the Ethics section of individual Chapters.


\section{Thesis Outline and Summary} \label{ch1:outline}
In this section, we provide an overview of the structure of the thesis and summarise the core themes of each Chapter. \dimos{The thesis consists of two main Parts, which complement each other: the first (\Autoref{part:part1}) introduces tools and resources, while the second (\Autoref{part:part2}) demonstrates their use and highlights their importance through a series of applications.}
Figure \ref{fig:thesis-outline} shows the entire structure of this thesis, where Chapters with the same interest are grouped together and Chapters that utilise tools are colour-coded the same way.

    \caption{Outline of the thesis. The Chapter number is indicated in brackets. The grey colour indicates connections between Chapters of Part 1 and Part 2. \dimos{Resources and models developed in Part 1 were used in Part 2.}}

\dimos{The first set of Chapters serves as preliminaries, introducing the foundations of \acp{LM}, their adaptation to social media, and the \ac{NLP} applications central to this thesis (Chapters \ref{ch:intro} and \ref{ch:background}).} The second set of Chapters focuses on identifying limitations in existing resources related to social media \ac{NLP} and proposing solutions. Mainly, we present our study for topic classification in \textit{Twitter} (\Autoref{ch:tweet-topic}), and our multilingual extension to this work (\Autoref{ch:x-topic}). Following this, we introduce an ensemble of models and datasets related to hate speech detection (\Autoref{ch:hate-speech}), and a benchmark for social media \ac{NLP} tasks (\Autoref{ch:super-tweeteval}), which builds upon our previous work.

The final part of the thesis focuses on applying the aforementioned resources to understand complex social interactions in social media. \Autoref{ch:political} introduces a study on the role of sentiment in political communication, followed by an analysis of misinformation spreaders (\Autoref{ch:spreaders}), and finally, we investigate how specific words can act as trigger points in online discussions (\Autoref{ch:triggers}). Finally, we conclude the thesis with a summary of the contributions and future work (\Autoref{ch:conclusion}).

As a temporal reference for this thesis, Figure \ref{fig:thesis-timeline} presents a high-level timeline of the publications that form part of this work, along with major events in the field of \ac{NLP}, such as the release of key models and significant developments like the shutdown of the Twitter API. Although this thesis primarily focuses on utilising smaller \acp{LM}, the work conducted after the release of ChatGPT and open-source models like LLaMA incorporates results from these models and often compares their performance as well.


\dimos{In the next Chapter (\Autoref{ch:background}), we provide the necessary preliminaries for this thesis. We discuss different \acp{LM} architectures and the need to adapt them for effective processing of social media data. We also introduce the \ac{NLP} tasks central to our research, including sentiment and emotion analysis, hate speech detection, misinformation analysis, and topic classification.}
