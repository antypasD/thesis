\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:intro} % Add a label in case you want to refer to the chapter.

The rise of social media platforms have transformed the way people communicate and interact online. Platforms like \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}, have grown to be an integral part of the lives of millions of people, with approximately 63\% of the population using a social media platform daily \footnote{https://www.statista.com/statistics/617136/digital-population-worldwide/}, and are seen as key sources of real-time information, public opinion, and social dynamics, making them valuable data sources for understanding human behavior and societal trends \cite{barbera2015birds,shu2017fake}. As populations shape opinions and influence each other through these platforms \cite{ausat2023role}, it has become crucial, from both business and scientific perspectives, to better understand how people interact and what factors drive their behaviors online.

This need becomes even more apparent in the context of political discourse, given how easily voters can be influenced and manipulated through targeted social media campaigns \citet{goodwin2023political}. For example, \citet{zhang2025trump} demonstrate how President Trump’s often incendiary posts on Twitter and Truth Social amplified social media user engagement and attracted increased news media attention. Similar studies on other major political events, such as Brexit \cite{mora2021influence}, highlight the pivotal role social media platforms play in shaping how information spreads through networks, facilitating content generation, and influencing user behaviour.

Despite their potential, analysing social media data poses numerous challenges for the field of \ac{NLP}. User-generated content on these platforms often lacks context and is highly informal, containing slang, emojis, abbreviations, typos, and other forms of noisy data. Moreover, language usage on social media evolves rapidly, making it difficult for static models and lexicons to keep pace. These characteristics demand robust, adaptive, and context-aware approaches to effectively process and interpret the vast and dynamic stream of social media text.


In order to tackle the limitations above, we explore existing methodologies and resources that work on understanding complex social interactions on social media. Subsequently, we introduce new models and datasets that improve on previous work, apply them on large corpora collected from \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit} to understand better human interactions regarding politics discourse, and misinformation and hate speech proliferation. The rest of this introductory chapter is organised as follows: \autoref{ch1:motivation} outlines the research aim and motivations for this thesis,  \autoref{ch1:contributions} outlines the contribution of research, \autoref{ch1:publications} lists publications that are peer-reviewed or currently under review, and \autoref{ch1:outline} describes the structure of the thesis and summarises this introductory chapter.

\section{Motivation} \label{ch1:motivation}
\ac{NLP} applications are essential for understanding human interactions in social media, especially in the context of political discourse and dissemination of misinformation and hate speech. For example, \ac{ML} models are used by social platforms, such as Facebook \cite{schroepfer2021update}, to detect harmful content and protect users from online abuse. Similarly, researchers have developed tools to analyse political communication on Twitter \cite{barbera2015birds}, track the spread of misinformation \cite{shu2017fake}, and understand the dynamics of online discussions \cite{zhang2018conversations}. 

Many hate speech detection models rely on supervised learning techniques, which require large amounts of annotated data to train effectively. However, the availability of high-quality annotated datasets for hate speech detection is limited, and existing datasets often suffer from biases and inconsistencies \cite{davidson2017automated}. This can lead to models that are not generalisable across different contexts or languages, limiting their effectiveness in real-world applications. Other applications, such as misinformation detection and sentiment analysis, also face similar challenges in terms of data availability and quality. For example, misinformation detection models often rely on large-scale datasets of social media posts, which can be difficult to obtain and may not accurately reflect the diversity of online content \cite{shu2017fake}. Similarly, sentiment analysis models often struggle to capture the nuances of social media language, which can vary significantly across different platforms and user demographics \cite{barbieri-etal-2022-xlm}.

Due to the heavily unstructured content often shared  in social media content, many of the aforementioned applications rely on specialised datasets to perform well. These datasets are often collected from social media platforms, such as \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}, and annotated by human coders to provide ground truth labels for training and evaluation. However, the quality and reliability of these datasets can vary significantly, depending on the annotation process and the expertise of the annotators \cite{klie2024analyzing}. This can lead to biases and inaccuracies in the models trained on these datasets, limiting their generalisability and effectiveness in real-world scenarios. 

The proliferation of social media platforms has created unprecedented challenges in political discourse and misinformation spread \cite{muhammed2022disaster}. Political polarization and the rapid dissemination of false information have become major societal concerns, affecting democratic processes and public opinion formation \cite{allcott2017social}. These challenges are further complicated by the heavily unstructured nature of social media content, which can pose difficulties even for larger models \cite{eisenstein2013bad,gain-etal-2023-reference}. Finally, it is crucial to recognise both the potential biases in existing models, tools, and datasets, as well as the environmental impact associated with developing and deploying \acp{LLM} \cite{bender2021dangers}.


\section{Contributions} \label{ch1:contributions}

This thesis aims to fill gaps in existing literature by procuring new resources and establishing methodologies specifically designed for social media applications. The research presented in this thesis focuses on two core areas: (I) developing and evaluating \ac{NLP} resources for social media analysis; and (II) applying these resources through well-defined approaches to analyse social media dynamics in the context of politics discourse, misinformation spread and hate speech propagation. By addressing these areas, the thesis aims to provide new insights into the dynamics of social media communication and contribute to the development of more effective tools for understanding and combating harmful online behaviours. To this end, the thesis makes a number of noticeable contributions as listed below:

\subsection{Part 1: \PARTONE}
The first core contribution of the thesis focuses on developing and evaluating \ac{NLP} tools specifically designed for social media applications. 

\begin{enumerate}[label=I.\arabic*]
    \item \textbf{New Datasets for Topic Classification on Twitter.} Extracting meaningful insights from the vast amount of data generated on Twitter is inherently challenging, and performing targeted topic analysis adds further complexity. Unsupervised methods such as topic modelling are often difficult to evaluate and interpret, while existing datasets for supervised classification are not well-suited for the informal and dynamic nature of social media content. To address this gap, we introduce two novel datasets, {\TweetTopic} and it is multilingual extension {\XTOPIC}, specifically designed for training and evaluating topic classification models on Twitter data. These datasets, along with corresponding \ac{SoTA} models, are detailed in Chapters~\ref{ch:tweet-topic} and~\ref{ch:xtopic}.
    \item \textbf{Unification of Hate Speech Detection Datasets.} We present a unified hate speech detection dataset specifically curated for social media content by leveraging and combining multiple existing resources. Although hate speech detection is a widely studied problem, many available datasets lack diversity and tend to focus on specific target groups, limiting the generalisability of trained models. By systematically aggregating and harmonising these resources, we construct a comprehensive dataset that, as demonstrated in \Autoref{ch:hate-speech}, enables models to achieve superior performance compared to those trained on individual datasets.
    \item \textbf{Introduction of a Benchmark for Twitter-related Tasks.} We present {\DATASET}, a benchmark that unifies and standardises the evaluation of social media \ac{NLP} tasks, addressing the current fragmentation in this area. Comprising XX diverse tasks, our benchmark poses significant challenges not only for smaller \acp{PLM} but also for the latest \acp{LLM}, as discussed in \Autoref{ch:super-tweeteval}.
\end{enumerate} 

A common observation across all three contributions is that smaller, specialised models, such as RoBERTa, can perform as well as, and often outperform, larger \acp{LLM} like GPT-4 on the evaluated tasks when fine-tuned. This observation is primarily due to the specialised training data: the models we evaluated are frequently trained on large corpora of social media content, making them better suited for domain-specific language use and nuances. Additionally, their architectural design contributes to this advantage. Unlike generative \acp{LLM}, which are based on a decoder architecture, smaller \acp{PLM} typically employ an encoder architecture, which is inherently more effective for discriminative tasks such as classification. Encoders focus on learning contextual representations of input sequences, which allows for precise feature extraction and robust classification performance which is particularly important for tasks like topic classification and hate speech detection, where subtle linguistic cues and contextual dependencies are critical.

The impact of this thesis is evidenced by the widespread adoption of the released datasets and models. For instance, the topic classification resources introduced in Chapters \ref{ch:tweet-topic} and \ref{ch:xtopic} have accumulated over 400,000 downloads on the Hugging Face platform\footnote{\url{https://huggingface.co/collections/cardiffnlp/tweettopic-65eb2a0eada92a05d3d103ce}}, highlighting their practical value and community uptake. Similarly, our hate speech classifier (\Autoref{ch:hate-speech}) has demonstrated performance comparable to, or surpassing, established tools such as Google’s Perspective API\footnote{\url{https://perspectiveapi.com/}} and OpenAI’s Moderation system\footnote{\url{https://platform.openai.com/docs/guides/moderation}}, as shown by \citet{shen2025hatebench}. It has also been adopted by researchers like \citet{arun2024x} to examine the temporal dynamics of hate speech on Twitter.


\subsection{Part 2: \PARTTWO}
We use the resources developed and insights extracted in the previous chapters to analyse complex social media dynamics.

\begin{enumerate}[label=II.\arabic*]
    \item \textbf{Politics and Negativity.} We conduct a large-scale analysis of posts by politicians from eight different parliaments across five languages (including English, Spanish, and Greek) to investigate how negatively charged tweets tend to spread more widely and rapidly than neutral or positive ones. To achieve this, we train domain-specific sentiment analysis models and develop a systematic approach to quantify and track the diffusion of such content. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:political}.
    \item \textbf{Profiling Misinformation Spreaders.} We present a methodology to profile users who frequently share misinformation on social media. By leveraging our previously developed models for topic classification and hate speech detection, along with sentiment and emotion analysis, we systematically analyse a large number of user accounts to uncover behavioural patterns and content characteristics associated with misinformation spread. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:spreaders}.
    \item \textbf{Trigger Points Analysis.} Trigger points are a relatively new concept referring to specific words or phrases that can provoke strong emotional reactions in discourse. We propose an approach for systematically identifying potential trigger points using various lexical features, focusing mainly on sentiment, emotion, and the presence of hate speech. Unlike previous qualitative studies that introduced the notion of trigger points, our approach adopts a quantitative perspective. Further details are provided in \Autoref{ch:triggers}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Ethics and Privacy}
The research included in the thesis has been approved by Cardiff University's ethics comitee and also follows the guidelines of ACL code of ethics\footnote{https://www.acm.org/code-of-ethics}.

Specifically, as our research focuses on socia media we have taken caution while collecting data used in our experiments to adhere to the aforementioned ethic rules. Firstly, all the data were collected using the official \acp{API} provided by \textit{Twitter} and \textit{Reddit}. Moreover, the privacy of the users whose data is used is protected by masking user handles and \acp{URL} to prevent any potential identification of users. Exceptions are data colleted from  verified\footnote{As defined by the legacy verified policy of Twitter in https://help.x.com/en/managing-your-account/legacy-verification-policy.} \textit{Twitter} users which are already public figures such as politicians, celebrites, and goverment employees.

Finally, regarding the annotations conducted, all coders were paid at least minimum wage for their work, and no personal identifying information was collected. This applies to the manual data labeling for \TweetTopic and \XTOPIC, as described in Chapters \ref{ch:tweet-topic} and \ref{ch:xtopic} respectively.
