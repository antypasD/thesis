This chapter provides the theoretical and methodological foundation of the thesis, covering the evolution of \ac{NLP} tools and their applications to social media data. \section{Language Models}
\acp{LM} are fundamental to \ac{NLP}, aiming to capture the statistical properties of language and predict the probability of a sequence of words. Their evolution has significantly impacted the capabilities of NLP systems, especially in handling complex human language.

\subsection{Traditional Language Models}

\subsubsection{Early Language Models}
Early \acp{LM} were often based on statistical methods, such as N-gram models, which predict the next word based on the previous N-1 words \cite{buck2014n}. These models, while foundational, suffered from limitations in capturing long-range dependencies and semantic meaning. To represent words, early systems frequently relied on static word embeddings such as Word2Vec \cite{mikolov2013efficient} and \ac{GloVe} \cite{pennington-etal-2014-glove}, where each word was mapped to a single, fixed vector regardless of context. While these representations improved over one-hot encodings by capturing some semantic similarity (e.g., “king” and “queen” being close in vector space), they failed to reflect how a word's meaning could shift depending on its usage in different contexts.

These limitations were overcomed, up to a degree, through the development of neural approaches capable of modeling sequential dependencies and context more effectively. Specifically, \acp{RNN} and their variants, such as \acp{LSTM} and \acp{GRU}, introduced a new class of language models capable of processing word sequences while maintaining a dynamic internal state \cite{yu2019review, dey2017gate}. These models improved the contextual understanding of language over static embeddings by updating representations as each token in a sequence was processed. However, they still faced challenges in handling long-term dependencies and lacked efficient parallelization during training, which restricted scalability and performance on large datasets \cite{sherstinsky2020fundamentals}.

\subsubsection{Masked Language Models}
\acp{MLM} emerged with the introduction of the Transformer architecture \cite{subakan2021attention}. Unlike traditional \acp{LM} that predict the next word, \acp{MLM} are trained by masking a percentage of the tokens in the input and then predicting those masked tokens based on the context provided by the unmasked tokens as seen in Figure \ref{fig:bert_architecture} \cite{BERT}. This bidirectional training allows them to learn richer, contextualized representations of words. These models are typically pretrained on massive corpora such as Wikipedia and the Common Crawl\footnote{\url{https://commoncrawl.org}}, enabling them to learn robust language representations.

For example, the embedding for the word bank in “he sat by the river bank” will differ significantly from that in “she went to the bank to deposit money,” because the surrounding context informs the model about the intended sense of the word. This is in stark contrast to earlier approaches like Word2Vec or \ac{GloVe}, which assign the same vector to bank regardless of its meaning in different contexts. These contextual embeddings allow MLMs to capture intricate semantic and syntactic relationships, making them highly effective across a wide range of downstream tasks.

Most contemporary \acp{MLM} are based on the \ac{BERT} architecture, which is an encoder-based model leveraging bidirectional self-attention mechanisms to capture contextual information from both the left and right of each token \cite{BERT}. Extensions to \ac{BERT} include \ac{RoBERTa}, which optimises the pre-training process and introduces larger datasets, longer training, and XLM-R, which extends \ac{RoBERTa} to multilingual settings \cite{liu2019roberta, conneau2019unsupervised}.

One of the main benefits of these models is that their knowledge can be utilised in various downstream tasks with minimal modifications. Also, due to their encoder architecture which allows them to understand context from both left and right, they display significantly improved performance when fine-tuned on tasks like text classification, question answering, and named entity recognition \cite{yang-etal-2023-glue}.\acp{MLM} have become the backbone of many state-of-the-art \ac{NLP} systems, enabling significant advancements in understanding and generating human language. They are particularly effective for tasks that require deep contextual understanding, such as sentiment analysis \cite{alaparthi2021bert}, hate speech detection \cite{mozafari2019bert}, and misinformation detection \cite{kaliyar2021fakebert}.

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Models}

Encoder-decoder models represent a distinct approach in language modeling, particularly effective for sequence-to-sequence tasks. These architectures consist of two components: (i) an encoder that processes the input sequence and compresses its information into a latent representation, and (ii) a decoder that generates an output sequence based on this representation. This architecture is well-suited for tasks where the output differs in form or length from the input, such as translation, summarization, and question generation. Similar to \acp{MLM}, encoder-decoder models are also be pretrained on large corpora, while leveraging self-attention mechanisms in both the encoder and decoder stacks which allows them to learn  bidirectional representations in the encoder while simultaneously training the decoder to generate sequences from noisy or incomplete inputs. 

Encoder-decoder models became popular with the introduction of \ac{T5} \cite{raffel2020exploring}. \ac{T5} casts  \ac{NLP} problems, such as summarisation, machine translation, and question asnwering, into a unified text-to-text format. It is pretrained on the \ac{C4} corpus\cite{JMLR:v21:20-074}  using a span corruption objective, where spans of text are masked and replaced with unique sentinel tokens, and the model learns to reconstruct the missing spans which enables it to develop robust representations for both understanding and generation (Figure \ref{fig:t5_architecture}).

    \centering

    \caption{Example of \ac{T5} finetuning for a summarisation task.}

An extension of \ac{T5}, \ac{Flan-T5} \cite{chung2024scaling}, further improves performance through instruction tuning \cite{wei2021finetuned} on a wide variety of tasks and datasets which enhances it's zero-shot and few-shot generalization capabilities. Another notable encoder-decoder model is \ac{BART} \cite{lewis2019bart}, which is trained on a denoising autoencoder objectivewhere the model learns to reconstruct original text from corrupted input. \ac{BART} combines the strengths of both \acp{MLM} and autoregressive models, making it effective for tasks like summarisation and translation.

Encoder-decoder models are widely used in tasks where the input and output differ significantly in structure or length, and similarly to \acp{MLM} they can be further finetuned for specific applications. Common use cases include machine translation, text summarization, question answering, and question generation. They can also be adapted for text generation tasks, producing coherent and contextually relevant text from prompts. However, these models are generally more complex and computationally intensive than \acp{MLM}, and they not always outperform encoder-only models on tasks that do not involve sequence generation \cite{gasparetto2022survey}. At the same time, they often lag behind decoder-only models, such as \acp{LLM}, in open-ended text generation tasks \cite{saattrup-nielsen-etal-2025-encoder}.


\subsection{Large Language Models}
\acp{LLM} represent the most recent advancement in language modeling, building upon the foundations laid by \acp{MLM} and encoder-decoder models. They are characterized by their massive scale, often containing hundreds of billions of parameters, and their ability to perform a wide range of tasks with remarkable performamce, often in a zero-shot or few-shot learning setting \cite{wang2020generalizing}. Unlike traditional \acp{LM}, which are typically trained on specific tasks or datasets, \acp{LLM} are pretrained on vast and diverse datasets encompassing text, code, and other modalities like images.

Architecturally, most \acp{LLM} are based on the Transformer decoder-only design and are trained using an autoregressive objective \cite{GPT3}where, given a sequence of tokens, the model learns to predict the next token in a left-to-right manner. In contrast to bidirectional models like \ac{BERT}, decoder-only models do not have access to future tokens during training, which makes them suited for generative tasks such as text completion, dialogue, summarization, and code generation.

Modern \acp{LLM} are capable to perform tasks based purely on natural language instructions, making \textit{prompt engineering} a critical aspect of their effective use \cite{reynolds2021prompt}. % Prompt engineering involves crafting input prompts that guide the model's behaviour toward producing the desired output. This technique has become increasingly important. The quality and structure of prompts can significantly affect model performance, especially in open-ended or multi-step reasoning tasks \cite{reynolds2021prompt}. 
Moreover, similarly to smaller \acp{LM} their performance on specific tasks can often be further improved through finetuning. This is typically applied to smaller versions of the models using techniques like \ac{QLoRA} \cite{dettmers2023qlora}, which enables efficient adaptation with limited computational resources.

Several notable \acp{LLM} have significantly advanced the field of \ac{NLP}. For example, OpenAI's GPT series, starting with GPT-2 \cite{radford2019language}, demonstrated the potential of large-scale unsupervised pretraining and autoregressive text generation. Its succesors, GPT-3 \cite{brown2020language} and GPT-4 \cite{achiam2023gpt} further scaled up the model size and training data, achieving impressive performance on a wide range of tasks with few-shot and zero-shot learning capabilities. 

Alongside proprietary models, several open-sourced \acp{LLM} have been introduceded. Meta's \ac{LLaMA} family \cite{touvron2023llama,touvron2023llama2} offers models that are computationally efficient and accessible to the research community, with versions as small as 1 billion parameters. Other prominent open-source alternatives include Mistral 7B and Mixtral \cite{jiang2024mixtral}—a mixture-of-experts model using sparse attention to activate only a subset of its parameters during inference—and Qwen \cite{qwen}, a high-performing model widely adopted in academia due to its open availability and strong benchmark results.

These models showcase that decoder-only architectures when scaled massively are able to achieve broad generalization across \ac{NLP} tasks with minimal supervision.

However, despite their impressive capabilities, \acp{LLM} pose significant challenges. These include high computational costs, issues related to interpretability, and the risk of generating biased or harmful content \cite{ferrara2023should,george2023environmental}. While decoder-only \acp{LLM} excel in generative and open-ended tasks, encoder-only models, such as those based on the \ac{BERT} architecture, remain highly competitive and often superior for certain structured tasks, like text classification and \ac{NER}, where their bidirectional context is advantageous. Moreover, encoder-based models tend to be more efficient to train and deploy for such applications.

Social media platforms have become central to the study of natural language in real world environments, offering vast amounts of real-time, user-generated content. These platforms differ significantly from traditional text sources such as books, news articles, or formal documents. Their informal, dynamic, and highly contextual nature presents unique opportunities and challenges for \ac{NLP} research and applications \cite{mustofa2025use}. A lot of these paltforms focus on different modalities, for example, Instagram emphasises photos and videos, while others like Snapchat are inherently multimodal. In contrast, Twitter and Reddit have emerged as particularly rich sources of linguistic data and are widely used in computational social science and NLP research \cite{}. This is primarily because text is the dominant medium of communication on these platforms, and they used to offer relatively easy access to data through official \acp{API} \footnote{Twitter's Academic \ac{API} shut down on XXX. Reddit's \ac{API} shutdown on XXX}. This thesis focuses on these two platforms due to their complementary structures and modes of interaction.

Twitter is structured around short, standalone messages called "tweets," which are constrained to 280 characters. Although tweets are restricted in length, they often convey substantial information and sentiment, enhanced by the use of hashtags, mentions, emojis, and embedded media. User interactions take the form of replies, retweets, and quote tweets, which together create loosely connected conversational threads (Figure \ref{fig:twitter_example}). The character limit encourages users to be linguistically inventive, resulting in the widespread use of slang, abbreviations, and non-standard grammatical constructions.

Reddit, in contrast, is a forum-based platform organized into topic-specific communities known as \textit{subreddits}. Posts on Reddit can be significantly longer than tweets and often initiate threaded discussions that allow for deeper, hierarchical conversations (\ref{fig:reddit_example}). This structure offers richer context and more coherent discourse patterns.

Although Reddit and Twitter differ in format and interaction design, they display several common linguistic characteristics as both are informal, rapidly evolving, and rely on similar engagement mechanisms such as upvotes and likes. These features shape the type and tone of user-generated content, making both platforms valuable for studying opinion formation, discourse dynamics, and online social behaviour.

The direct application of \acp{LM} trained on formal or curated text corpora often fails to generalize effectively to social media data. Social media text presents a wide range of linguistic challenges for \ac{NLP} due to its informal and dynamic nature. It often features slang, abbreviations (e.g., "brb", "imo"), and non-standard grammar, which deviate from conventional language norms. Posts frequently lack sufficient context, leading to ambiguity in interpretation, particularly when sarcasm or irony is involved. Additionally, platform-specific metadata such as emojis, hashtags, and mentions play an important role in conveying meaning and identifying topics. Multilinguality and code-switching are also common, with users often blending languages or dialects within a single post. Language on these platforms evolves rapidly, with new slang and trends emerging constantly, requiring models to adapt over time. Social media content also reflects societal biases and may include toxic or harmful language, posing ethical and technical challenges. Finally, the short and often noisy nature of posts, particularly on platforms like Twitter, often requires careful pre-processing or tailored model architectures. 

These challenges have led to the development of specialised language models that are pre-trained or fine-tuned on large-scale social media corpora to capture the idiosyncrasies of online discourse \cite{nguyen2020bertweet}.

These models are explicitly designed to handle the noise and variability inherent in social media and they often incorporate strategies for addressing spelling errors, abbreviations, emojis, and platform-specific syntax (e.g., hashtags, mentions, retweet markers). By learning from in-domain data, these models are better equipped to understand the linguistic norms, stylistic features, and emergent expressions characteristic of social platforms.

Popular examples of specialised language models include: (i) BERTweet \cite{nguyen2020bertweet}, a RoBERTa-based model trained exclusively on 850M English tweets; (ii) Twitter-RoBERTa \cite{loureiro-etal-2022-timelms}, which continues training from the RoBERTa checkpoint using a large tweet corpora; (iii) 
Bernice \cite{delucia2022bernice} a multilingual social media model designed to handle cross-lingual and code-switched content; and  (iv) DialoGPT \cite{zhang2019dialogpt} A conversational response generation model built by fine‑tuning GPT‑2 on approximately 147 million Reddit comment exchanges. These examples reflect a common trend: RoBERTa is the backbone architecture for many social media-specific models. This is largely due to its robustness, relatively low computational requirements for large-scale pretraining, and the fact that its main limitation, a short maximum context length of 512 tokens, is not a major drawback for short-form social media text. Specialized models trained on domain-specific social media data consistently outperform their general-purpose counterparts and, in some tasks, match or surpass \acp{LLM} in downstream applications such as hate speech detection, stance classification, and misinformation detection \cite{}.

\subsection{Hate Speech Detection}

binary, multiclass, multilabel

Hate speech detection is a critical application of NLP on social media, aiming to identify content that expresses hatred or encourages violence towards individuals or groups based on attributes like race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexual orientation, etc. This is a highly sensitive and challenging task due to its subjective nature and the evolving tactics of hate speech perpetrators.

Hate speech detection involves identifying offensive, threatening, or discriminatory language. Prior work uses keyword matching, lexical features, and deep learning-based classifiers. Recent benchmarks such as HatEval and datasets like HASOC provide resources for multilingual and cross-platform evaluation.

Challenges on Social Media:

    Ambiguity and Context: Distinguishing genuine hate speech from offensive language, sarcasm, or legitimate criticism.

    Evolving Language: Hate speech uses slang, coded language (dog whistles), and novel expressions to evade detection.

    Imbalance in Data: Hate speech is typically a minority class, leading to skewed datasets and model bias towards the majority (non-hate speech) class.

    Subjectivity of Definitions: What constitutes "hate speech" can vary across jurisdictions and cultural contexts.

    Multilingualism: Hate speech manifests differently in various languages and cultures.



\subsection{Misinformation \& Polarisation}

detection, fact checkinng/verification

The spread of misinformation (unintentional falsehoods) and disinformation (intentional falsehoods) and its role in fostering polarisation on social media platforms pose significant societal challenges. NLP plays a crucial role in analyzing and combating these phenomena.

The spread of misinformation on social media contributes to public harm, especially in contexts like health communication and political discourse. Identifying false or misleading content is complicated by subtle rhetorical strategies and the rapid evolution of narratives.

Polarisation further exacerbates misinformation spread, as users form echo chambers and engage in selective exposure. Language models can help identify linguistic signals of polarisation and frame detection. However, challenges remain in detecting emerging misinformation, handling multimodal content, and mitigating algorithmic biases.


Misinformation and Disinformation Analysis:

Detection: Identifying false or misleading content. This often involves:

        Fact-checking: Verifying claims against reliable sources (challenging to automate fully).

        Stance Detection: Determining if a text agrees, disagrees, discusses, or is unrelated to a given claim.

        Propaganda Detection: Identifying rhetorical techniques used to manipulate public opinion.

        Source Credibility: Assessing the reliability of the source of information.

        Linguistic Features: Analyzing linguistic patterns (e.g., sensationalist language, emotional intensity, specific vocabulary) often associated with misinformation.

    Propagation Analysis: Understanding how misinformation spreads across networks, often leveraging network science alongside NLP to analyze user interactions, sharing patterns, and community structures.

    Debunking and Intervention: Developing NLP-powered systems to identify and flag misinformation, or to generate counter-narratives and provide accurate information.

Polarisation Analysis:

    Echo Chambers and Filter Bubbles: NLP can identify communities with shared beliefs and analyze how information flows within and between these groups.

    Opinion Dynamics: Tracking how opinions evolve over time and how they are influenced by social interactions and exposure to different narratives.

    Stance and Ideology Detection: Classifying users or content based on their political or social leanings.

    Linguistic Markers of Polarisation: Analyzing specific language choices (e.g., divisive rhetoric, "us vs. them" framing, emotional language) that indicate or contribute to polarization.

    Disagreement and Deliberation: Studying how online discussions devolve into unproductive arguments versus constructive dialogue.

Challenges in Misinformation \& Polarisation:

    "Truth" Definition: The ground truth for misinformation can be ambiguous or rapidly change.

    Evolving Tactics: Misinformation campaigns constantly adapt their language and dissemination strategies.

    Subtlety: Misinformation can be subtle, relying on implications or omissions rather than outright falsehoods.

    Emotional Resonance: Misinformation often leverages strong emotions, making it highly shareable.

    Cross-platform Coordination: Campaigns often span multiple social media platforms.

    Attribution: Identifying the source and intent behind misinformation is complex.

    Ethical Implications: The risk of suppressing legitimate dissent or creating "thought police" when developing automated detection systems.

Approaches:

    Hybrid Models: Combining NLP techniques with network analysis, computer vision (for multimodal misinformation), and knowledge graphs.

    Adversarial Training: Developing models robust to subtle manipulations designed to evade detection.

    Explainable AI (XAI): Making models more transparent to understand why they classify certain content as misinformation or polarizing.

    Large Language Models: Their ability to reason, summarize, and understand complex contexts is being explored for tasks like fact-checking assistance and generating counter-narratives. However, LLMs can also generate convincing misinformation, highlighting the dual-use nature of the technology.
