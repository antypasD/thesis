\section{Language Models}
\label{ch:background:language_models}
\acp{LM} are fundamental to \ac{NLP}, aiming to capture the statistical properties of language and predict the probability of a sequence of words. Their evolution has significantly impacted the capabilities of NLP systems, especially in handling complex human language.

% \subsection{Pre-Transformers Language Models}
\subsection{Early Language Models}
Early \acp{LM} were often based on statistical methods, such as N-gram models, which predict the next word based on the previous N-1 words \cite{buck2014n}. These models, while foundational, suffered from limitations in capturing long-range dependencies and semantic meaning. To represent words, early systems frequently relied on static word embeddings such as Word2Vec \cite{mikolov2013efficient} and GloVe \cite{GLOVE}, where each word was mapped to a single, fixed vector regardless of context. While these representations improved over one-hot encodings by capturing some semantic similarity (e.g., “king” and “queen” being close in vector space), they failed to reflect how a word's meaning could shift depending on its usage in different contexts.

These limitations were overcomed, up to a degree, through the development of neural approaches capable of modelling sequential dependencies and context more effectively. Specifically, \acp{RNN} and their variants, such as \acp{LSTM} and \acp{GRU}, introduced a new class of language models capable of processing word sequences while maintaining a dynamic internal state \cite{yu2019review, dey2017gate}. These models improved the contextual understanding of language over static embeddings by updating representations as each token in a sequence was processed. However, they still faced challenges in handling long-term dependencies and lacked efficient parallelisation during training, which restricted scalability and performance on large datasets \cite{sherstinsky2020fundamentals}.

\subsection{Masked Language Models}
\acp{MLM} emerged with the introduction of the Transformer architecture \cite{subakan2021attention}. Unlike traditional \acp{LM} that predict the next word, \acp{MLM} are trained by masking a percentage of the tokens in the input and then predicting those masked tokens based on the context provided by the unmasked tokens, as seen in Figure \ref{fig:bert_architecture} \cite{BERT}. This bidirectional training allows them to learn richer, contextualised representations of words. These models are typically pre-trained on massive corpora such as Wikipedia and the Common Crawl\footnote{\url{https://commoncrawl.org}}, enabling them to learn robust language representations.

For example, the embedding for the word bank in “he sat by the river bank” will differ significantly from that in “she went to the bank to deposit money,” because the surrounding context informs the model about the intended sense of the word. This is in stark contrast to earlier approaches like Word2Vec or GloVe, which assign the same vector to bank regardless of its meaning in different contexts. These contextual embeddings allow MLMs to capture intricate semantic and syntactic relationships, making them highly effective across a wide range of downstream tasks.

    \centering
     \scalebox{0.8}{
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/chapter2/bert_architecture.jpg}}
    \caption{Example of \ac{MLM} objective for training in BERT.}
    \label{fig:bert_architecture}
\end{figure}

Most contemporary \acp{MLM} are based on the BERT architecture, which is an encoder-based model leveraging bidirectional self-attention mechanisms to capture contextual information from both the left and right of each token \cite{BERT}. Extensions to BERT include RoBERTa, which optimises the pre-training process and introduces larger datasets, longer training, and XLM-R, which extends RoBERTa to multilingual settings \cite{ROBERTA, conneau2019unsupervised}.

One of the main benefits of these models is that their knowledge can be utilised in various downstream tasks with minimal modifications. Also, due to their encoder architecture, which allows them to understand context from both left and right, they display significantly improved performance when fine-tuned on tasks like text classification, question answering, and named entity recognition \cite{yang-etal-2023-glue}. \acp{MLM} have become the backbone of many state-of-the-art \ac{NLP} systems, enabling significant advancements in understanding and generating human language. They are particularly effective for tasks that require deep contextual understanding, such as sentiment analysis \cite{alaparthi2021bert}, hate speech detection \cite{mozafari2019bert}, and misinformation detection \cite{kaliyar2021fakebert}.

\subsection{Encoder-Decoder Models}

Encoder-decoder models represent a distinct approach in language modelling, particularly effective for sequence-to-sequence tasks. These architectures consist of two components: (i) an encoder that processes the input sequence and compresses its information into a latent representation, and (ii) a decoder that generates an output sequence based on this representation. This architecture is well-suited for tasks where the output differs in form or length from the input, such as translation, summarisation, and question generation. Similar to \acp{MLM}, encoder-decoder models are also be pre-trained on large corpora, while leveraging self-attention mechanisms in both the encoder and decoder stacks, which allows them to learn bidirectional representations in the encoder while simultaneously training the decoder to generate sequences from noisy or incomplete inputs. 

Encoder-decoder models became popular with the introduction of T5 \cite{T5}. T5 casts  \ac{NLP} problems, such as summarisation, machine translation, and question answering, into a unified text-to-text format. It is pre-trained on the \ac{C4} corpus\cite{T5}  using a span corruption objective, where spans of text are masked and replaced with unique sentinel tokens, and the model learns to reconstruct the missing spans which enables it to develop robust representations for both understanding and generation (Figure \ref{fig:t5_architecture}). 

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \scalebox{0.8}{\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/chapter2/t5_architecture.png}}
    \caption{Example of T5 finetuning for a summarisation task.}
    \label{fig:t5_architecture}
\end{figure}

An extension of T5, Flan-T5 \cite{chung2024scaling}, further improves performance through instruction tuning \cite{wei2021finetuned} on a wide variety of tasks and datasets, which enhances its zero-shot and few-shot generalisation capabilities. Another notable encoder-decoder model is BART \cite{lewis2019bart}, which is trained on a denoising autoencoder objective where the model learns to reconstruct original text from corrupted input. BART combines the strengths of both \acp{MLM} and autoregressive models, making it effective for tasks like summarisation and translation.

Encoder-decoder models are widely used in tasks where the input and output differ significantly in structure or length, and similarly to \acp{MLM} they can be further finetuned for specific applications. Common use cases include machine translation, text summarisation, question answering, and question generation. They can also be adapted for text generation tasks, producing coherent and contextually relevant text from prompts. However, these models are generally more complex and computationally intensive than \acp{MLM}, and they do not always outperform encoder-only models on tasks that do not involve sequence generation \cite{gasparetto2022survey}. At the same time, they often lag behind decoder-only models, such as \acp{LLM}, in open-ended text generation tasks \cite{saattrup-nielsen-etal-2025-encoder}.


\subsection{Large Language Models}
\acp{LLM} represent the most recent advancement in language modelling, building upon the foundations laid by \acp{MLM} and encoder-decoder models. They are characterised by their massive scale, often containing hundreds of billions of parameters, and their ability to perform a wide range of tasks with remarkable performance, often in a zero-shot or few-shot learning setting \cite{wang2020generalizing}. Unlike traditional \acp{LM}, which are typically trained on specific tasks or datasets, \acp{LLM} are pre-trained on vast and diverse datasets encompassing text, code, and other modalities like images.

Architecturally, most \acp{LLM} are based on the Transformer decoder-only design and are trained using an autoregressive objective \cite{GPT3}where, given a sequence of tokens, the model learns to predict the next token in a left-to-right manner. In contrast to bidirectional models like BERT, decoder-only models do not have access to future tokens during training, which makes them suited for generative tasks such as text completion, dialogue, summarisation, and code generation.

Modern \acp{LLM} are capable of performing tasks based purely on natural language instructions, making \textit{prompt engineering} a critical aspect of their effective use \cite{reynolds2021prompt}. % Prompt engineering involves crafting input prompts that guide the model's behaviour toward producing the desired output. This technique has become increasingly important. The quality and structure of prompts can significantly affect model performance, especially in open-ended or multi-step reasoning tasks \cite{reynolds2021prompt}. 
Moreover, similarly to smaller \acp{LM}, their performance on specific tasks can often be further improved through finetuning. This is typically applied to smaller versions of the models using techniques like \ac{QLoRA} \cite{dettmers2023qlora}, which enables efficient adaptation with limited computational resources.

Several notable \acp{LLM} have significantly advanced the field of \ac{NLP}. For example, OpenAI's GPT series, starting with GPT-2 \cite{GPT2}, demonstrated the potential of large-scale unsupervised pretraining and autoregressive text generation. Its successors, GPT-3 \cite{GPT3} and GPT-4 \cite{achiam2023gpt}, further scaled up the model size and training data, achieving impressive performance on a wide range of tasks with few-shot and zero-shot learning capabilities. 

Alongside proprietary models, several open-sourced \acp{LLM} have been introduced. Meta's LLAMA family \cite{touvron2023llama,touvron2023llama2} offers models that are computationally efficient and accessible to the research community, with versions as small as 1 billion parameters. Other prominent open-source alternatives include Mistral 7B and Mixtral \cite{jiang2024mixtral}—a mixture-of-experts model using sparse attention to activate only a subset of its parameters during inference—and Qwen \cite{qwen}, a high-performing model widely adopted in academia due to its open availability and strong benchmark results.

These models showcase that decoder-only architectures, when scaled massively, are able to achieve broad generalisation across \ac{NLP} tasks with minimal supervision.

However, despite their impressive capabilities, \acp{LLM} pose significant challenges. These include high computational costs, issues related to interpretability, and the risk of generating biased or harmful content \cite{ferrara2023should,george2023environmental}. While decoder-only \acp{LLM} excel in generative and open-ended tasks, encoder-only models, such as those based on the BERT architecture, remain highly competitive and often superior for certain structured tasks, like text classification and \ac{NER}, where their bidirectional context is advantageous. Moreover, encoder-based models tend to be more efficient to train and deploy for such applications.


\section{Social media platforms}
Social media platforms have become central to the study of natural language in real-world environments, offering vast amounts of real-time, user-generated content. These platforms differ significantly from traditional text sources such as books, news articles, or formal documents. Their informal, dynamic, and highly contextual nature presents unique opportunities and challenges for \ac{NLP} research and applications \cite{mustofa2025use}. A lot of these platforms focus on different modalities; for example, Instagram emphasises photos and videos, while others like Snapchat are inherently multimodal. In contrast, Twitter and Reddit have emerged as particularly rich sources of linguistic data and are widely used in computational social science and \ac{NLP} research \cite{antonakaki2021survey,proferes2021studying}. This is primarily because text is the dominant medium of communication on these platforms, and they used to offer relatively easy access to data through official \acp{API} \footnote{Twitter's Academic \ac{API} shut down on March 2023. Reddit's \ac{API} shutdown in June 2023}. This thesis focuses on these two platforms due to their complementary structures and modes of interaction.

\subsection{Specialised Language Models for Social Media}

The direct application of \acp{LM} trained on formal or curated text corpora often fails to generalise effectively to social media data \cite{sun-etal-2024-toward}. Social media text presents a wide range of linguistic challenges for \ac{NLP} due to its informal and dynamic nature. It often features slang, abbreviations (e.g., "brb": "be right back", "imo": "in my opinion"), and non-standard grammar, which deviates from conventional language norms \cite{bashlovkina2023smile}. Posts frequently lack sufficient context, leading to ambiguity in interpretation, particularly when sarcasm or irony is involved \cite{khan2025textual}. Additionally, platform-specific metadata such as emojis, hashtags, and mentions play an important role in conveying meaning and identifying topics. Multi-linguality and code-switching are also common, with users often blending languages or dialects within a single post. Language on these platforms evolves rapidly, with new slang and trends emerging constantly, requiring models to adapt over time. Social media content also reflects societal biases and may include toxic or harmful language, posing ethical and technical challenges. Finally, the short and often noisy nature of posts, particularly on platforms like Twitter, often requires careful pre-processing or tailored model architectures. 

These challenges have led to the development of specialised language models that are pre-trained or fine-tuned on large-scale social media corpora to capture the idiosyncrasies of online discourse \cite{bertweet}.

These models are explicitly designed to handle the noise and variability inherent in social media and they often incorporate strategies for addressing spelling errors, abbreviations, emojis, and platform-specific syntax (e.g., hashtags, mentions, retweet markers). By learning from in-domain data, these models are better equipped to understand the linguistic norms, stylistic features, and emergent expressions characteristic of social platforms.

Popular examples of specialised language models include: (i) BERTweet \cite{bertweet}, a RoBERTa-based model trained exclusively on 850M English tweets; (ii) Twitter-RoBERTa \cite{loureiro-etal-2022-timelms}, which continues training from the RoBERTa checkpoint using a large tweet corpora; (iii) 
Bernice \cite{delucia2022bernice} is a multi-lingual social media model designed to handle cross-lingual and code-switched content; and  (iv) DialoGPT \cite{zhang2019dialogpt} is A conversational response generation model built by fine‑tuning GPT‑2 on approximately 147 million Reddit comment exchanges. These examples reflect a common trend: RoBERTa is the backbone architecture for many social media-specific models. This is largely due to its robustness, relatively low computational requirements for large-scale pre-training, and the fact that its main limitation, a short maximum context length of 512 tokens, is not a major drawback for short-form social media text. Specialised models trained on domain-specific social media data consistently outperform their general-purpose counterparts and, in some tasks, match or surpass \acp{LLM} in downstream applications such as hate speech detection, stance classification, and misinformation detection \cite{monnar2024cross}.

\subsection{Sentiment Analysis}
Sentiment analysis aims to determine the emotional tone or overall sentiment expressed in a piece of text. It is commonly framed as a multi-class classification task, where a model classifies a given text as negative, neutral, or positive based on its sentiment \cite{mejova2009sentiment}. Alternative approaches include aspect-based sentiment analysis, where the model assesses the sentiment of a text towards a specific target or entity \cite{zhang2022survey}. Sentiment analysis can also be modelled as an ordinal regression task, where the model assigns a score on a predefined scale (e.g. 0 to 5), with 0 representing the most negative and 5 the most positive sentiment \cite{saad2019twitter}.

On social media, sentiment analysis is crucial for understanding public opinion toward brands, products, events, or political figures. For example, \citet{matalon2021using} applied sentiment analysis to examine opinion inversion in political tweets related to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Similarly, \citet{rodriguez2021sentiment} analysed Twitter posts mentioning Spanish political parties to study sentiment dynamics over time. \citet{alaparthi2021bert} used sentiment analysis to investigate public attitudes toward COVID-19 vaccines on Twitter.

Other researchers have explored the use of sentiment analysis in brand monitoring and customer feedback. \citet{chowdhury2024sentiment} proposed a framework for analysing customer reviews on social media to help businesses understand customer satisfaction and improve their products or services. Similarly, \citet{chamlertwat2012discovering} demonstrated how sentiment analysis can be used to extract consumer insights from social media data, enabling companies to adapt their strategies based on public opinion.

Sentiment analysis has also been employed to assess public reactions to major events, such as natural disasters or political elections. For instance, \citet{willson2021twitter} used sentiment analysis to examine Twitter reactions to the 2019–2020 Australian bushfires, revealing regional and temporal variations in sentiment. Similarly, \citet{behl2021twitter} applied sentiment analysis to tweets related to the COVID-19 pandemic, helping identify user concerns and needs during the crisis and illustrating how social media can inform disaster response strategies.

In this thesis, sentiment analysis, framed as a multi-class problem,  is used extensively to extract insights into users' opinions and attitudes on various topics discussed on social media. This is particularly significant in  \Autoref{ch:political}, where we introduce a custom domain-specific classifier, and in Chapters \ref{ch:spreaders} and \ref{ch:triggers}, where we employ \acp{PLM} fine-tuned on social media data.

\subsection{Emotion Detection}
Emotion detection, while similar to sentiment analysis it is a more complex and challenging task as it aims to identify more granular human emotions rather than overall negative, neutral, and positive emotional states. Specifically, an emotion detection model provided a text will highlight specific emotions like anger, joy, sadness, fear, surprise, disgust, etc that are present in it. Emotion detection can be framed as a multi-class classification task \cite{balabantaray2012multi}, where each class corresponds to a specific emotion, or as a multi-label classification task \cite{zhang2020emotion}, where multiple emotions can be detected simultaneously in a single text. It can also be approached as an ordinal regression task, where the model assigns a score to each emotion (e.g. 0-5) based on its intensity or relevance to the text \cite{muhammad-etal-2025-brighter}.

Emotion detection is important for social media analysis, particularly relevant when there is a need for a more fine-grained understanding of emotional responses to events or content on social media. Researchers have utilised emotion detection to study political discourse, marketing campaigns, and hate speech studies, among others. For example, \citet{bhaumik2023adapting} applies emotion detection techniques on Twitter posts related to the 2017 French presidential elections and studies the effect of emotional content as influence indicators in online political discussions. Researchers \cite{rahib2022emotion} have also used emotion detection to explore the effect of the COVID-19 pandemic to the general population and study how they are responding to and dealing with evolving circumstances. Finally, \citet{schafer2023hs} investigate the connection between hate speech and emotions and propose a method for jointly modelling both phenomena.

In this thesis, emotion detection, framed as a multi-label task, is utilised in Chapters \ref{ch:spreaders}, and \ref{ch:triggers} as part of our analysis to extract insights from users about their emotional status.

\subsection{Topic Modelling}
Topic modelling focuses on identifying the underlying themes or topics present in a collection of texts. It is often framed as an unsupervised learning task, where the model discovers latent topics without predefined labels. Common approaches include \ac{LDA} \cite{lda}, \ac{NMF} \cite{lee1999learning}, and more recent methods like BERT-based topic models such as BertTopic \cite{bertopic} and Top2Vec \cite{angelov2020top2vec}. These methods aim to group similar documents based on their content, allowing for the identification of prevalent themes within a corpus.

The unsupervised nature of topic modelling, while beneficial in many cases, can also be a limitation when specific topics or categories are of interest. In such cases, supervised topic classification is often used, where a model is trained on labelled data to predict predefined topics for new texts. This approach is particularly useful when performing targeted analysis where specific categories, such as politics, technology, and sports, are of interest. This is particularly important on social media, as it helps in organising vast amounts of information, identifying trending discussions that are of interest and filtering content based on specific themes. Applications of topic classification on social media focus on identifying trending topics \cite{lee2011twitter,bouadjenek2022longitudinal} but have also been utilised often in parallel with sentiment analysis to gain insights into public opinion and discourse \cite{huang2013sentiment,rodrigues2022new}.

In this thesis, we introduce a multi-label topic classification dataset and model in \Autoref{ch:tweet-topic}, along with a multilingual extension in \Autoref{ch:x-topic}, tailored for social media applications. The aim is to support community profiling and to identify topics of interest for specific user groups, as we demonstrate in \Autoref{ch:spreaders}.

\subsection{Hate Speech Detection}
% binary, multiclass, multilabel
Hate speech detection involves identifying offensive, threatening, or discriminatory language. It is a critical application of \ac{NLP} on social media, aiming to identify content that expresses hatred or encourages violence towards individuals or groups based on attributes like race, ethnicity, religion, gender, and sexual orientation. 
This is a highly sensitive and challenging task that is crucial for moderation and creating safer online environments. Hate speech detection can be framed as a binary classification task, where the model predicts whether a given text contains hate speech or not \cite{saleh2023detection} but can an also be approached as a multi-class classification task where the model identifies specific types of hate speech (e.g., racism, sexism, homophobia) \cite{qureshi2021compromised}, or as a multi-label classification task, where multiple forms of hate speech can be present in a single text \cite{dirting2022multi}.

Hate speech detection is particularly important on social media platforms, where the rapid spread of harmful content can have significant real-world consequences, as was the case during the Rohingya crisis in Myanmar \cite{nourooz2023transitional}. Researchers have applied hate speech detection to various social media platforms, including Twitter and Reddit, to study the prevalence and impact of hate speech in online discourse. For example, \citet{kim2021misinformation} investigates relations between misinformation and hate speech, specifically in the case of the COVID-19 pandemic and hate speech targeted at Asian communities. Other researchers \cite{agarwal2021hate} have explored the nature and prevalence of hate speech in political dialogue on Twitter, and also studies that focus on specific expressions of hate speech like misogyny and abuse of female politicians on Twitter \cite{fuchs2021normalizing}.

In this thesis, hate speech detection is framed as a multi-class classification task. We perform a cross-dataset analysis of existing hate speech resources, and introduce a new robust dataset and model in \Autoref{ch:hate-speech}. This model is applied in Chapters \ref{ch:spreaders} and \ref{ch:triggers} to identify harmful content and to analyse the dynamics of online hate speech.

\subsection{Misinformation \& Polarisation}

Misinformation and disinformation analysis is a critical area of \ac{NLP} research, particularly in the context of social media platforms where the rapid spread of false or misleading information can have significant societal impacts. The spread of misinformation on social media contributes to public harm, especially in contexts like health communication and political discourse. Identifying false or misleading content is complicated by subtle rhetorical strategies and the rapid evolution of narratives.


Misinformation analysis encompasses several interrelated tasks, each addressing a different dimension of how false or misleading content is produced, disseminated, and interpreted. One of the primary tasks in this area is misinformation detection, typically framed as a binary classification task aimed at distinguishing between accurate and misleading content \cite{oshikawa-etal-2020-survey, aljawarneh2022fake}. While early models achieved encouraging results, recent work highlights ongoing challenges such as adapting to evolving linguistic patterns and handling diverse forms of misinformation, including satire and rumours \cite{taylor2024misinformation}. Building on this, stance detection extends the analysis by examining whether a text agrees, disagrees, discusses, or remains unrelated to a given claim, thereby capturing the nuances of how audiences engage with contested information \cite{hardalov2022survey, karande2021stance}. Such approaches are particularly valuable for tracing shifts in public opinion, with recent work demonstrating improved cross-topic generalisation when applied to unseen claims \cite{aljrees2023fake}. Another line of research focuses on propaganda detection, which aims to uncover strategies designed to manipulate audiences \cite{da2020survey, faye-etal-2024-exposing}. Early efforts explored sentence-level cues \cite{cruz2019sentence}, while more recent approaches adopt fine-grained, multi-label classification to capture the presence of multiple propaganda techniques within a single text \cite{ahmad2025enhanced}. Finally, linguistic feature analysis provides a broader perspective by examining stylistic and lexical markers characteristic of misinformation, such as sensationalist language, heightened emotional intensity, and distinctive vocabulary choices \cite{chang2025sens}. Studies in this area, for example, show how sentiment polarity, readability, and other linguistic cues can serve as reliable indicators that differentiate misinformation from factual reporting \cite{choudhary2021linguistic}.

Stance detection, on the other hand, determines whether a text agrees, disagrees, discusses, or is unrelated to a given claim, and can be framed as a multi-class classification task \cite{hardalov2022survey}. Propaganda detection focuses on identifying rhetorical techniques used to manipulate public opinion, often framed as a multi-class classification task where the model identifies specific propaganda techniques used in a text \cite{da2020survey}. Finally, linguistic feature analysis involves analysing linguistic patterns (e.g., sensationalist language, emotional intensity, specific vocabulary) often associated with misinformation, which can be framed as a feature extraction task where the model identifies specific linguistic features in a text \cite{chang2025sens}. 


On the other hand, the study of polarisation seeks to understand how societies become divided into opposing camps \cite{del2016echo}. This division is often reinforced by echo chambers and selective exposure, where individuals primarily encounter information that aligns with their pre-existing views. Such dynamics can exacerbate biases, facilitate the spread of misinformation, and undermine constructive discourse \cite{wang-etal-2025-decoding}.

From an \ac{NLP} perspective, polarisation can be approached through a variety of tasks. One line of work focuses on echo chambers and filter bubbles, where methods are used to identify tightly knit communities with shared beliefs and to analyse how information circulates within and across these groups \cite{minici2022cascade}. Another direction is opinion dynamics, which involves tracking how opinions shift over time in response to social interactions and competing narratives \cite{willaert2020building}. Research has also explored linguistic markers of polarisation, studying the use of divisive rhetoric, “us versus them” framing, and emotional language that signal or intensify polarisation \cite{herdiansah2018religious}. Finally, studies on disagreement and deliberation investigate the conditions under which online discussions escalate into unproductive arguments as opposed to fostering constructive dialogue \cite{stromer2009agreement}.

In this thesis, we look at both misinformation and polarisation effects. Rather than focusing on tasks such as misinformation detection or fact verification, our analysis emphasises how misinformation spreads and who shares it, specifically in \Autoref{ch:spreaders}, where we investigate user profiles likely to disseminate false information. Furthermore, in Chapters \ref{ch:political} and \ref{ch:triggers}, we focus on polarisation in social discourse, examining political communication and the dynamics that escalate online discussions.

Sentiment and emotion analysis support the extraction of public insights and emotional responses in Chapters \ref{ch:political}, \ref{ch:spreaders}, and \ref{ch:triggers}. We address the lack of large, in-domain datasets for social media by introducing multi-label and multilingual topic classification resources in Chapters \Autoref{ch:tweettopic} and \ref{ch:x-topic}, tailored for community profiling and the identification of topics of interest for specific user groups, as later demonstrated in \Autoref{ch:spreaders}. Similarly, the hate speech resources introduced in  \Autoref{ch:hate-speech} are designed to offer a more robust and generalisable alternative to existing datasets, and are applied in Chapters \ref{ch:spreaders} and \ref{ch:triggers} to study the presence and dynamics of harmful discourse. These resources also form the basis of the {\DATASET} benchmark introduced in \Autoref{ch:super-tweeteval}, which provides a challenging evaluation framework for recent \acp{LM} on social media–focused tasks. Finally, misinformation and polarisation are examined through the identification of linguistic characterstics of likely misinformation spreaders in \Autoref{ch:spreaders} and the analysis of polarised discourse in Chapters \ref{ch:political} and \ref{ch:triggers}.
