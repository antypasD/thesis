% Part 1
\part{\PARTONE}\label{part:part1}
The first part of this thesis focuses on the creation and evaluation of essential resources for \ac{NLP} in the context of social media. The unique linguistic and contextual characteristics of online discourse, such as the prevalence of informal language, emojis, and rapid topic evolution, create challenges for general-purpose NLP systems. To address this fragmentation, this part of the work introduces a series of domain-specific datasets, models, and benchmarks designed to capture the nuances of social media text. 

These resources, which include large-scale datasets for topic classification (\ref{ch:tweet-topic} and \ref{ch:x-topic}), a comprehensive cross-dataset evaluation for hate speech detection (\Autoref{ch:hate-speech}), and a unified benchmark for social media tasks (\Autoref{ch:super-tweeteval}), address the important resource gaps and enables researchers to perform more robust social media analysis. \Autoref{part:part1} grounds the work in theory and provides practical tools that are used in  in the empirical investigations introduced \Autoref{part:part2}, ensuring that they are technically rigorous, replicable, and community-oriented.

The chapters included in this part (\ref{ch:tweet-topic}–\ref{ch:super-tweeteval}) are reproduced verbatim from their published versions, with only minor modifications to presentation.

\part{\PARTTWO}\label{part:part2}
In the previous part of this thesis, we introduced a set of domain-specific datasets, models, and benchmarks tailored for analysing social media text. These resources address core \ac{NLP} tasks such as topic classification, hate speech detection, and sentiment analysis, and were designed to capture the linguistic and contextual characteristics unique to online discourse. In \Autoref{part:part2}, we focus on applying these resources in large-scale, real-world analyses that address the research questions outlined in Chapter \ref{ch:intro}.

In \Autoref{part:part2}, we focus on applying these resources in large-scale, real-world analyses that address key research questions. By connecting methodological contributions to real-world phenomena, specifically by examining the relationship between sentiment and virality in political tweets (\Autoref{ch:political}), the linguistic patterns of misinformation spreaders (\Autoref{ch:spreaders}), and the concept of "trigger points" that escalate online discussions (\Autoref{ch:triggers}), this part of the thesis demonstrates the broader value of the approaches developed. It highlights how computational models and datasets can be utilized to provide insights into pressing societal challenges, and situates the work within the wider dialogue between \ac{NLP} and computational social science.

Similar to \Autoref{part:part1}, the chapters included in this part (\ref{ch:political}–\ref{ch:triggers}) are reproduced verbatim from their published versions, with only minor modifications to presentation.
